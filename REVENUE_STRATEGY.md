# Jorbex Revenue Strategy & Long-Term Valuation Analysis

> An honest assessment of the current monetisation model, its ceilings, and the path to becoming the definitive gateway to Africa's talent.

## The Honest Assessment

---

## What Works Well

### The geo-pricing is smart

Charging ₦3,000 in Nigeria but $25 in the US is the right move. It makes the product affordable across Africa while extracting full value from diaspora employers and international companies hiring African talent. Most platforms get this wrong by pricing in USD everywhere and killing adoption in-market.

### Paystack + Stripe dual routing is a genuine competitive advantage

Most African SaaS products force everyone through Stripe, which means African employers hit friction — no local payment methods, foreign card issues, conversion fees. Supporting USSD, mobile money, and bank transfers via Paystack removes the single biggest payment drop-off point for African SMEs.

### Candidates are free

This is correct. You need supply (talent) before you can sell access to that supply. Free candidates = fast supply growth = valuable employer product.

### Recurring billing (now implemented) is essential

The old one-time payment model would have made revenue unpredictable and churn invisible. Proper subscriptions give you MRR, which is what investors value.

---

## What Is Currently a Ceiling, Not a Floor

### Flat fee is limiting at scale

Right now one employer pays ₦3,000/month whether they hire 0 people or 50. That means your revenue is purely a function of employer count — not value delivered. As you grow, you'll leave significant money on the table.

The platforms that win in this space (LinkedIn, Workday, BambooHR) layer pricing:

| Model | What it unlocks |
|-------|----------------|
| Flat subscription | Gets you started — you have this ✓ |
| Per-job-post or per-hire fee | Scales with employer activity |
| Candidate featured profiles / boosts | New revenue from the supply side |
| Government / enterprise contracts | 10–100x the SME price |
| API access for HR software integrations | B2B2B revenue |
| Payroll processing fees | % of payroll processed through the calculator |

### The payroll calculator is an undermonetised asset

Right now it's a free tool. But if you extend it into actual payroll processing — employers run payroll through Jorbex, you charge a percentage or per-employee fee — that's a fundamentally more valuable and sticky product than job posting access.

### Assessment proctoring has standalone value

Large companies (banks, telecoms, government ministries) pay significant money for verified skills testing. Jorbex already has face-detection proctoring built. That is a product you could sell separately to HR departments and training institutions as a service, completely independent of job posting.

## On Becoming the Gateway to Africa's Talent

### The infrastructure is right. The network effect is the hard part.

The platform has the technical foundations that matter:

- Multi-country, multi-currency ✓
- Multi-language (EN / FR / AR / PT covers most of Africa) ✓
- Skills assessments with integrity controls ✓
- Payroll tools that build employer trust ✓
- Records and employment history (creates stickiness) ✓

But a talent marketplace is a **two-sided network**. The value compounds only when both sides are large enough that employers find candidates fast and candidates find jobs fast. Until that density exists in each market, the platform is a product looking for a network.

### The platforms that achieved this in Africa

Jobberman (Nigeria), BrighterMonday (East Africa), and Careers24 (South Africa) got there through:

1. **Deep local market focus first** — one country, dominant, then expand
2. **Partnerships with universities and vocational training institutions** (supply side)
3. **Government and NGO partnerships** (legitimacy + bulk contracts)
4. **Employer brand content** that keeps employers coming back between hires

## Valuation Implications

Investors value recurring SaaS on a revenue multiple — typically **5–12x ARR** for early-stage African tech, **15–30x ARR** for high-growth. What drives the multiple up:

| Factor | Current state | What would improve it |
|--------|--------------|----------------------|
| MRR predictability | Now good (recurring billing fixed) | Churn data after 3–6 months |
| Net revenue retention | Unknown | Add upsells so employers spend more over time |
| Geographic moat | Strong (local currencies, local languages) | Deepen 2–3 markets before spreading thin |
| Data asset | Growing | Candidate skill scores become a proprietary dataset |
| Enterprise pipeline | None visible | One government contract changes the story |

The combination of **skills assessment + proctoring + employment records + payroll** is genuinely differentiated. Most job boards are just listings. Jorbex has the infrastructure to be an **employment operating system** — which is a much more defensible and valuable position.

---

## Bottom Line

**Is the current monetisation profitable?**
It can be — the unit economics are sound if employer CAC (cost to acquire) stays below approximately 3 months of subscription revenue. That depends entirely on marketing efficiency.

**Does it give long-term revenue?**
The recurring model does. But the flat fee will cap it. The next version of the pricing should add a usage layer — per hire, per assessment, or per payroll run.

**Can it become the gateway to Africa's talent?**
The infrastructure says yes. The network effect is the work that remains. Winning 2–3 markets deeply — Nigeria + Kenya + South Africa, or Nigeria + Francophone West Africa — before spreading across 54 countries is the path that works.

## The Highest-Value Move Not Yet Built

A **verifiable digital credential** — when a candidate passes a Jorbex assessment, that score is attached to their profile permanently. Employers globally learn to trust the Jorbex score the way they trust a university degree.

That is the product that makes Jorbex genuinely irreplaceable, and it is only possible because the proctoring infrastructure already exists.
